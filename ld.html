Lucid Dreaming

Origins, dream control, proof, benefits

Lucid dreaming refers to the conscious perception that one is dreaming. In this state, the dreamer may often have some degree of control over his/her own actions within the dream or even the characters and setting of the dream. Dream control has been reported to improve with practiced deliberate lucid dreaming, but the ability to control aspects of the dream is not necessary for a dream to qualify as "lucid.” A lucid dream is merely any dream during which the dreamer knows they are dreaming.

The quality of lucid dreaming varies from low to high. High lucidity suggests that the dreamer is well aware that what they are currently experiencing, and possibly controlling, is not real; perceived danger, as a result, seems to disappear, reducing the dreamer’s hesitance and opening up a world of possibilities. A pre-lucid dream is one in which the dreamer considers the question, "Am I asleep and dreaming?" The dreamer may or may not come to the correct conclusion. Such experiences are likely to occur to people who are deliberately cultivating the ability to lucid dream, but may also occur spontaneously to those with no prior intention to achieve lucidity while dreaming.

Scientific Evidence

The occurrence of lucid dreaming has been scientifically verified. In 1975, psychologist Keith Hearne successfully recorded a communication from a dreamer experiencing a lucid dream, by observing the subject move his eyes left and right in a previously agreed upon pattern after becoming lucid. Years later, Stanford researcher Stephen LaBerge conducted similar work by using eye signals to map the subjective sense of time in dreams and by comparing the electrical activity of the brain while singing awake and while dreaming.

Benefits

Lucid dreaming lets you make use of the dream state that comes to you every night to have a stimulating reality.
- Stephen LaBerge

Even though lucid dreaming is not tantamount to full dream control, a plethora of benefits remains. Lucid dreaming can improve creativity and memory, facilitate personal growth, help safely conquer fears and phobias, and even curb nightmares. Experienced lucid dreamers report creating dream plots in advance, meeting and conversing with their heroes, processing grief by reuniting with lost loved ones, practicing and improving real life skills, experiencing the exhilarating freedom of flying, and finding personal meaning in life.
