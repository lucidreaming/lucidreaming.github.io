How to Learn

Techniques, routines, speed

Lucid dreaming is by no means an innate skill. The first technique to learn how to lucid dream is to practice dream recall. Put some paper next to your bed and record your dream immediately upon waking. A few lines or so for the first week will turn into multi-page recall ability as you develop this skill.

The next step is to use your dream log to identify common and/or recurring dream elements. These elements are then utilized for “reality checks,” those moments when you ask yourself, “Am I dreaming?” If you believe you are, you can then begin testing, which entails making a change to your behavior or identifying inconsistencies in the dreamscape. Since working memory can generally hold only seven plus or minus two bits of information, and you are constantly forming your dreamscape in real-time, certain elements such as written text, times, or complex patterns may change if you look away and then look back at them.

There are a number of techniques that help induce lucidity. One such technique tested by LaBerge, referred to as Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreaming (MILD), involves waking up in the middle of the night, setting the intention to lucid dream, and then focusing repeatedly on this intention while you go back to sleep.

The first few times you achieve lucidity, you will likely be so excited that you will wake yourself up. Two tested techniques for extending lucidity are spinning in place and looking at your hands. If you are lucky and have a tattoo on your hand, if it is mislocated or in any way distorted, you will know you are dreaming.
